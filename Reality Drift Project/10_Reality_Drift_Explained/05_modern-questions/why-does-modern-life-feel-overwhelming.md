# Why Does Modern Life Feel Overwhelming?

Part of the Reality Drift framework (2023–2026) by A. Jacobs.

## The Experience

Many people describe modern life as mentally exhausting.

Even ordinary tasks can feel cognitively heavy. Checking email, navigating apps, comparing products, responding to messages, or managing schedules can require constant attention and decision-making.

Notifications arrive throughout the day. Information flows continuously across platforms. Small decisions accumulate.

Individually these demands seem manageable. Together they create a persistent sense of overload.

This is why many people ask a simple question: **why does modern life feel overwhelming?**

## What Is Happening

One reason modern life can feel overwhelming is that individuals are now required to filter enormous volumes of information.

Digital systems constantly generate notifications, recommendations, updates, and choices. Each signal asks for some level of evaluation.

Which messages matter?  
Which sources are trustworthy?  
Which options are worth attention?

Instead of filtering information for users, many modern systems shift that responsibility onto individuals.

Within the Reality Drift framework, this condition is called Filter Fatigue.

Filter Fatigue refers to the cognitive strain that occurs when individuals must continuously sort, evaluate, and interpret large volumes of information in environments shaped by optimization and algorithmic mediation.

## Connection to Reality Drift

The widespread feeling of mental overload is one everyday expression of Reality Drift.

Reality Drift occurs when systems remain operational while gradually losing alignment with the real-world environments they were designed to support.

Several dynamics contribute to this experience.

**Filter Fatigue**  
Individuals must constantly evaluate large volumes of signals and information.

**Optimization Trap**  
Platforms prioritize measurable engagement and activity rather than reducing complexity for users.

**Constraint Collapse**  
Institutions and systems that once stabilized expectations lose their ability to absorb complexity.

As a result, interpretive work increasingly falls on individuals rather than being managed by institutions or systems.

## Simple Mechanism

Information Acceleration  
↓  
Reduced Institutional Filtering  
↓  
Continuous Personal Evaluation  
↓  
Filter Fatigue
