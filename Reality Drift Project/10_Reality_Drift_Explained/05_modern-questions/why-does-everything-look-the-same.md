# Why Does Everything Look the Same Now?

Part of the Reality Drift framework (2023–2026) by A. Jacobs.

## The Experience

Many people have noticed a strange pattern across modern culture: **everything seems to look the same.**

Coffee shops in different cities share the same interior styles. Restaurants use the same fonts and menus. Instagram posts follow similar poses and captions. TikTok videos repeat the same formats and editing styles.

Even products, branding, and architecture often appear surprisingly similar across companies and industries.

This creates a strange cultural feeling. Things are constantly changing, yet much of it still looks identical.

Many people describe this as a sense that culture has become repetitive or flattened.

## What Is Happening

This pattern often emerges when cultural production becomes increasingly shaped by optimization.

Platforms, creators, and businesses are rewarded for visibility, engagement, and reach. When certain aesthetics, formats, or styles perform well, they are quickly copied and repeated.

Successful patterns spread rapidly because they are easier to recognize and easier for algorithms to distribute.

Over time this creates a feedback loop where high-performing styles become dominant.

Within the Reality Drift framework, this process is closely related to what is called the Optimization Trap.

The Optimization Trap occurs when systems increasingly optimize for measurable performance indicators — such as engagement, clicks, and reach — rather than for deeper meaning or diversity of expression.

## Connection to Reality Drift

The widespread feeling that culture looks increasingly similar is one everyday example of **Reality Drift**.

Reality Drift describes a condition in which systems remain operational while gradually losing alignment with the realities they were originally meant to reflect.

Several dynamics contribute to this pattern.

**Optimization Trap**  
Algorithms and platforms reward content that performs well according to measurable metrics.

**Synthetic Realness**  
Cultural experiences are designed to appear authentic and relatable, even when they follow standardized templates.

**Filter Fatigue**  
As the volume of content increases, recognizable patterns become easier for audiences to process and evaluate.

Together these dynamics gradually compress cultural variety into a smaller set of highly visible formats.

## Simple Mechanism

Algorithmic Optimization  
↓  
Replication of High-Performing Patterns  
↓  
Cultural Convergence  
↓  
Perceived Sameness
