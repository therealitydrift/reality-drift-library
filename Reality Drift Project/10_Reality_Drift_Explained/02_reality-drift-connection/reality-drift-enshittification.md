## Overview

**Enshittification** is a term used to describe a pattern in which digital platforms gradually degrade in quality as their incentives shift over time.

The concept often describes a lifecycle in which platforms initially prioritize user experience in order to grow, then begin prioritizing advertisers, investors, or internal metrics as the system matures.

As these incentive structures evolve, platforms may increasingly optimize revenue, engagement, or growth metrics even when those optimizations reduce the quality of the user experience.

Over time this process can produce environments that feel more constrained, more commercialized, or less aligned with the interests of the people using the system.

Within the **Reality Drift framework**, this pattern reflects deeper structural dynamics shaping the evolution of modern digital platforms.

---

## How Enshittification Relates to Reality Drift

Within the **Reality Drift framework**, enshittification can be understood as a pattern that emerges when systems increasingly optimize measurable platform incentives rather than the original goals those platforms were designed to serve.

As platforms mature, they may fall into an **Optimization Trap**, where metrics such as engagement, growth, or advertising performance become the primary indicators guiding design decisions.

To sustain these metrics, platforms often reshape interfaces, recommendation systems, and content visibility rules in ways that maximize measurable performance signals.

These changes can gradually alter the structure of digital environments, increasing the volume of promoted content, algorithmically prioritized material, and engagement-driven features that users must navigate.

As these optimized systems expand, users may experience increasing **Filter Fatigue**, as they must continuously sort through recommendations, advertisements, notifications, and platform-generated prompts competing for attention.

Over time the platform can remain highly functional according to internal metrics while the experience it produces becomes progressively shaped by platform incentives rather than by the needs or intentions of users.

Within the framework, this condition can be understood as a form of **Reality Drift**. 

---

## Where This Pattern Appears

These dynamics can appear across many modern digital environments:

- Social media platforms that increasingly prioritize sponsored or algorithmically promoted content  
- Digital marketplaces where platform rules shift to favor monetization strategies  
- Online platforms introducing additional advertisements, subscription tiers, or engagement mechanisms  
- Recommendation systems that prioritize platform growth metrics over user experience  

In these environments, systems may remain highly active and profitable while the experiences they produce gradually drift away from the conditions that originally made the platform useful or appealing.
