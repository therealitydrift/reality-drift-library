# Why Does Time Feel Like It's Speeding Up?

Part of the Reality Drift framework (2023–2026) by A. Jacobs.

## The Experience

Many people feel that time seems to be moving faster than it used to.

Weeks pass quickly. News cycles change constantly. Cultural trends appear and disappear in rapid succession. Even personal milestones can feel compressed together.

People often describe a sense that the pace of life has accelerated.

Days feel full, yet strangely blurred. Events happen quickly but do not always leave a clear impression in memory.

This leads many people to ask a familiar question: **why does time feel like it's speeding up?**

## What Is Happening

One reason time can feel accelerated is that modern environments expose individuals to an unusually high rate of information and change.

News, social media, messaging platforms, and digital services continuously update throughout the day. Each update competes for attention.

Instead of moving through a relatively stable environment, people now navigate streams of rapidly changing signals.

Within the Reality Drift framework, this contributes to Filter Fatigue.

Filter Fatigue occurs when individuals must constantly sort, evaluate, and interpret large volumes of information. As attention becomes fragmented across many signals, experiences are processed more quickly and often with less contextual depth.

When experiences are compressed this way, the passage of time can feel faster.

## Connection to Reality Drift

The sense that time is accelerating is another everyday expression of Reality Drift.

Reality Drift occurs when systems remain operational while gradually losing alignment with the environments they were originally designed to support.

Several dynamics contribute to the changing experience of time.

**Information Acceleration**  
Digital systems produce continuous updates that compress how experiences are perceived and processed.

**Filter Fatigue**  
Individuals must constantly evaluate signals, reducing the depth of attention given to any single moment.

**Optimization Trap**  
Platforms compete to capture attention, increasing the speed and volume of content.

Together these pressures create environments where events arrive faster than attention can fully stabilize them.
