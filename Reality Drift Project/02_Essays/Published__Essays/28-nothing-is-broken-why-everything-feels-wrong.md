# Article 28

## Nothing Is Broken. So Why Does Everything Feel Wrong?

*Originally published April 30, 2026*

Hand-drawn suburban street scene in muted watercolor tones, showing a man standing at the edge of a quiet neighborhood sidewalk. The houses, trees, and mailboxes appear normal and well-kept, but the central lamppost leans noticeably over the path while everything else remains upright. The scene feels coherent at a glance, yet subtly misaligned on closer inspection, illustrating Reality Drift—where everyday environments continue to function while their alignment with reality quietly degrades.

There’s a specific kind of feeling that’s been spreading, and most people don’t have language for it. It doesn’t come from things going wrong in an obvious way. In many cases, it emerges from people and organizations operating with the right intentions, improving systems, optimizing processes, and scaling what works, until something begins to slip.

Nothing is obviously broken. You can move through your day, complete tasks, and interact with the tools around you. From the outside, everything appears to be working. And yet something feels off, not in a dramatic way, but in a subtle, persistent way. Like things are still running, but no longer fully connected to what they were originally grounded in.

This is what it looks like when systems continue to operate, and often even improve by their own internal metrics, while gradually losing alignment with the reality they were meant to represent. Once you see it, you start seeing it everywhere.

## Twelve Signs You’re Living Inside Reality Drift

### 1. Everything Still Works, But It Feels Harder Than It Should

Something that should be simple becomes less straightforward over time. You buy a new computer, add a mouse, keyboard, and headphones, and expect them to work together seamlessly. Instead, you encounter small incompatibilities, connection limits, and overlapping systems that don’t fully align. Each device works on its own terms, but not always in coordination. The task remains simple in principle, but the experience of completing it becomes more fragmented.

### 2. You’re Paying More, But Getting Less

The product stays recognizable, but what it offers begins to shift. Package sizes are reduced slightly, designs are refreshed, and existing variations are reintroduced as new offerings. Each change is small and justifiable on its own, but the cumulative effect is a reduction in what the product actually provides, without a clear moment where the change becomes obvious.

### 3. Maintaining the System Starts to Matter More Than Using It

You log in to do one thing, but end up dealing with everything around it instead. Resetting passwords, verifying your identity, updating payment details, fixing access issues, navigating account settings. None of these are the reason you came to the system, but they start to take up more of your time. The actual use case gets squeezed between these tasks, and it begins to feel like you’re maintaining access to the system more than actually using it.

### 4. Things Improve on Paper, But Not in Practice

On paper, things improve. In practice, it can feel different. You can see this clearly in airline travel, where the optimization trap becomes visible. Flying still works, but the experience has changed. What used to be included is now broken into layers. You pay to pick your seat, pay for bags, pay for anything beyond the bare minimum. Even small things feel gated. The flight happens, but the experience feels fragmented, like something that used to be whole has been taken apart.

### 5. Interaction Replaces Resolution

Customer support systems have become highly accessible in a surface-level sense. You can initiate a conversation quickly, receive immediate responses, and move through structured workflows. However, these interactions often fail to produce meaningful resolution. Instead, they route you through predefined paths that maintain the appearance of responsiveness without effectively addressing the underlying problem.

### 6. Digital Systems Spill Into the Physical World

At Safeway, you’re asked to pull out your phone, open an app, and scan QR codes to get basic discounts. In theory, it’s simple. In practice, it breaks the flow. Your cellular signal is weak, the lighting makes the code hard to scan, or you’re standing there trying to get it to work while someone else is waiting to grab yogurt and you’re blocking the way.

Nothing is fundamentally broken, but the experience becomes more complicated than it needs to be. A simple task now requires coordinating between physical and digital systems that don’t fully align and no longer feel natural to the environment.

### 7. Trust Moves to the System, Not the Experience

Platforms like Airbnb make this visible. Listings look better than ever, high-quality photos, optimized descriptions, near-perfect ratings. But instead of increasing trust, that consistency starts to create doubt. Everything looks too clean, too aligned, taking on a kind of synthetic realness. You spend more time reading between the lines, searching for what’s missing. Still, you rely on it to decide. Even as trust weakens, the system becomes the only way to evaluate the experience.

### 8. Complexity Reaches the Point Where You Stop Trying to Understand

This shows up clearly in healthcare. At one point, a diligent person might try to understand how it works, estimating costs, comparing plans, figuring out deductibles, copays, and coverage details. There’s an assumption that if you put in the effort, you can make informed decisions. But over time, the system becomes too complex to fully grasp. Costs are difficult to predict, rules vary depending on context, and even basic questions don’t have clear answers. You can spend hours trying to understand it and still feel uncertain. Instead of trying to understand the system, you start questioning whether it’s worth the effort at all.

### 9. You Start Adapting to the System Instead of It Adapting to You

Buying a high-end TV should be simple. You spend thousands expecting a seamless experience, but the interface is slow, buggy, or poorly designed. Instead, you buy a $50 streaming stick, switch inputs, and bypass the built-in software entirely. Over time, this starts to feel normal. You stop expecting the product to function as it should and instead adjust how you use it. The better experience exists, but outside the system you paid for. Instead of the system adapting to you, you adapt to its limitations.

### 10. Things Begin to Converge Toward the Same Form

Consider something like buying ice cream. You’ll find eight different versions of chocolate ice cream, including fudge swirl, triple chocolate, and chocolate brownie, all presented as distinct. But when you actually try them, the differences are minimal. The names change, the packaging shifts, but the underlying flavor barely moves. What looks like variety gradually converges toward the same form, creating a kind of filter fatigue where you’re forced to choose between options that are effectively the same.

### 11. Language and Conversations Start to Converge

Terms like “premium,” “optimized,” or “personalized” appear everywhere, but they stop pointing to anything concrete. Different products, services, and experiences are described in the same way, even when they deliver very different outcomes. The language remains familiar, but its meaning becomes harder to pin down.

At the same time, conversations begin to sound similar. The same talking points, the same phrasing, the same interpretations of events show up across different people. It feels less like independent perspectives and more like overlapping outputs shaped by the same feeds, the same content loops, and the same algorithmically mediated environments.

### 12. Things Can Be Wrong Without Consequence

You upgrade your home heating system and are pushed toward a heat pump. It’s heavily promoted, backed by government incentives, and positioned as more efficient and cost-saving. The decision looks obvious on paper. The data supports it, the rebates reinforce it, and the system pushes you toward that choice.

But after installation, the real-world performance doesn’t match the expectation. The efficiency isn’t what was advertised, and the cost savings don’t fully materialize. When you ask why, the responsibility diffuses. HVAC companies point to government data. The rebate programs defer to the installers. Each part of the system functions, but no one is accountable for the outcome. The system continues to promote the same solution, even as the real-world results fall short.

## The Pattern Beneath It All

These patterns emerge from a shared structural condition. Systems that operate at scale rely on compressed representations of reality. Metrics, models, and language allow them to function efficiently, but they also introduce distance. As those representations become the primary objects of optimization, the connection to the underlying reality they were meant to represent begins to weaken.

Diagram of the Reality Drift framework showing a cycle between Filter Fatigue, Optimization Trap, and Synthetic Realness, illustrating how modern systems remain functional while drifting out of alignment with reality.

The defining feature of this pattern is that it does not produce immediate failure. If systems were breaking outright, the problem would be visible and urgent. Instead, they remain usable, coherent, and in many cases technically improved. This allows the misalignment to accumulate gradually without making it obvious that something has gone wrong.

The absence of failure is not proof of alignment. It is often the condition that allows drift to continue unnoticed.
