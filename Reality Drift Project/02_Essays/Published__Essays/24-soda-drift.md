# Article 24

## Soda Drift: When Market Optimization Flattens Variety

*Originally published February 23, 2026*

> “Optimization favors the average. Over time, the average replaces everything else.”

Illustrated grocery shelf filled with varied soda cans, representing product standardization, market optimization, and fidelity decay within consumer systems — a visual metaphor for Reality Drift.

I was talking recently with my younger brother when we somehow got onto the topic of soda, and we started reminiscing about the “healthy” sodas we used to get as kids when our mom shopped at natural grocery stores. Back then, in the late 90s and early 2000s, these stores were still independent, varying from region to region and full of products you wouldn’t see in mainstream supermarkets. They hadn’t yet been absorbed by larger chains like Whole Foods, and certainly not by Amazon. When it came to soda, there were basically two options: Blue Sky and Hansen’s.

My brother mentioned that he had looked for them a few years ago and discovered they were no longer available. He told me Coca-Cola had bought Blue Sky and effectively killed the product line, while Pepsi had done the same with Hansen’s. It sounded plausible, the kind of familiar corporate story in which large companies notice an emerging shift toward healthier products and eliminate early competitors before they can grow too strong.

## No Single Decision

After our conversation, I looked into it myself. His version wasn’t entirely wrong, but the reality turned out to be more complex and, in some ways, more revealing. Hansen’s had actually acquired Blue Sky back in 2000. In the early 2000s, the company experienced explosive success with a new product line: Monster Energy. Over time, that success reshaped the entire business. By 2012, Hansen’s had even rebranded itself as Monster Beverage Corporation, reflecting a strategic pivot away from its original natural soda roots.

As part of that shift, Hansen’s discontinued its natural soda line in 2015. Around the same time, Blue Sky was sold to Coca-Cola, which continued producing it for several years before discontinuing it entirely in 2021. There was no dramatic corporate takedown and no coordinated effort to eliminate healthier alternatives. The outcome emerged gradually through a series of ordinary business decisions, each responding to incentives already present within the system.

## Optimization Always Outruns Its Original Limits

In modern markets, optimization pressures increasingly reshape which products can survive, and ultimately the structure of the market itself. As a result, the overall complexity of market offerings gradually flattens over time. Where consumers once navigated meaningful differences between regional brands, ingredient philosophies, and production approaches, a product category like soda becomes increasingly compressed into standardized markers such as brand visibility, price positioning, marketing narratives, and distribution reach. Over time, the map of the market begins to replace the territory, and consumers interact less with underlying qualities and more with representations such as labels, advertising, shelf placement, and the purchasing patterns that determine what stores choose to stock.

A promising alternative to mainstream soda, made with real cane sugar, fewer artificial ingredients, and less aggressive branding, simply faded from store shelves. Seen from a systems perspective, this was not just a story about soda, but a small example of how modern systems drift. In their early years, natural sodas like Hansen’s and Blue Sky operated under very different constraints, shaped by smaller distribution networks, niche consumer demand, and retail environments that allowed for meaningful variation. Their survival depended on staying closely aligned with the people who actively sought them out. As the market expanded, the conditions that once supported that relationship began to shift.

What happened to these sodas isn’t unique. In general, there are more products today in a literal sense, yet meaningful variety has steadily narrowed. What emerges instead is a form of **surface diversity, structural convergence**, where offerings appear different while being shaped by the same underlying constraints. In food systems, for example, the need for long shelf life, easy transport, uniform appearance, and predictable yields often comes at the expense of qualities that are harder to standardize, such as flavor complexity, freshness, and regional specificity. Over time, what remains are the features that can travel easily, scale reliably, and fit within large distribution systems, producing an environment where choices multiply, yet increasingly feel interchangeable.

In the soda industry, this shift unfolded as the market scaled. Large beverage systems came to revolve around growth, distribution efficiency, shelf dominance, and the ability to generate predictable demand at scale. Over time, systemic pressures increasingly pulled toward what appeared to be the needs of an “average” consumer, a figure defined more by aggregated data than by lived experience. In that environment, products like natural soda did not require a single decision to eliminate them; they simply became harder to sustain within a system oriented toward scale.

The effects of this dynamic may appear modest in a soda aisle, yet the same underlying mechanism now shapes far more consequential systems. Optimization toward aggregated averages increasingly influences how healthcare is delivered, how educational systems are structured, and how digital environments are designed. In each case, the system continues to function while its orientation gradually shifts away from the specific conditions and lived realities that once grounded it.

## Losing Fit Over Time

The soda aisle still looks full, the brands are familiar, and production is more efficient than ever. Yet something subtle has thinned out over time. The kinds of products that once reflected specific tastes, priorities, and communities gradually gave way to offerings shaped for scale. Today, it’s hard to point to a single moment when this change happened. It just unfolded slowly, until the conditions that once allowed alternatives like Hansen’s and Blue Sky to exist were no longer there.
