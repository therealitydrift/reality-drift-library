# Article 18

## When Optimization Exhausts Meaning

*Originally published December 24, 2025*

**“The system improves its metrics while losing its purpose.”**

![An office worker sits at a desk surrounded by towering stacks of perfectly organized paperwork and forms. The endless bureaucracy illustrates how systems of optimization can overwhelm the human mind.]

Something is shifting inside big tech, and the dashboards aren’t capturing it. User engagement among Millennials and Gen X is flattening or declining. Not catastrophically, but just enough to feel wrong. People are disengaging in subtle ways. They open apps out of habit rather than intention, scroll without curiosity, post more selectively, and feel themselves caring less.

Internally, this shows up as a puzzle. Externally, it shows up as exhaustion. The dominant explanation is that this is cyclical, driven by generational shifts, market saturation, and changing tastes. But that explanation focuses on user preferences rather than platform design. In reality, platform incentive structures continue optimizing engagement metrics long after those metrics stopped representing real human experience.

This is a UX problem, but not the kind that can be fixed with better research, cleaner interfaces, or smarter experimentation. It reflects a threshold being crossed between lived human experience and the simplified representations systems are built to measure. Engagement may begin to flatten, yet broader performance metrics can still appear strong enough to justify continued refinement. The user experience degrades before the numbers register a crisis. That gap between measurable performance and lived meaning is where reality drift begins.

## When Optimization Outruns Meaning

Most modern digital systems operate under the assumption that if a metric once correlated with value, then pushing that metric higher will create more value. But that assumption eventually breaks. Optimization requires compression. Compression reduces complex reality into measurable proxies. At first, those proxies track something meaningful such as attention, engagement, or retention. Once those measures become targets, system design shifts toward improving the metric rather than the experience it represents. This is Goodhart’s Law in action. When a measure becomes a target, it stops reliably tracking the thing it was meant to measure.

In UX, this distortion becomes the optimization trap. At a certain point, chasing stronger performance indicators can actively make the experience worse. Engagement can be driven through friction, compulsion, or novelty even when users are no longer satisfied. AI-driven personalization and ad efficiency can mask a deteriorating experience for a while, but they cannot restore meaning. On dashboards, the system may appear healthy, while users on the other side of the data feel increasingly depleted. Because these feedback loops are delayed, the damage compounds before it becomes visible. As systems continue to prioritize measurable engagement, experiential quality declines, and that decline drives further optimization.

## Filter Fatigue and the Death by a Thousand Decisions

To understand why this erosion feels so visceral, you have to look at the cognitive cost imposed on users, in the mundane daily moments.

Modern life is saturated with micro-decisions.

Which ad do you pick during a Hulu commercial break?

Which of the ten nearly identical condiments all on sale at the grocery store do you choose?

Which steps, accounts, permissions, updates, and compatibility checks are remaining to simply set up your new phone or computer?

None of these decisions are hard on their own. But they accumulate and eventually produce exhaustion as people are forced to constantly compress, prioritize, and ignore information in high-entropy environments. By the time they reach social media, the place they go to relax, their minds are already overloaded from nonstop filtering. What they encounter is not connection or delight, but more sorting, signaling, and self-regulation. Even leisure becomes labor, and the result is numbness.

Filter fatigue also changes how platforms interpret user behavior. When attention is depleted, behavior flattens. People click what is easiest, linger without interest, and react without intention. To ranking algorithms and engagement models, these actions still register as valid engagement. But they are no longer expressions of meaning. They are artifacts of exhaustion. This is the point where optimization crosses a line, as recommendation systems begin responding primarily to behavior shaped by fatigue rather than to deliberate intent.

## From Lived Experience to Synthetic Realness

When systems calibrate themselves to behavior shaped by fatigue and overload, they begin reinforcing those distortions at scale. The feed can remain engaging and responsive, even as it reflects a narrowed version of human experience. What appears aligned on the screen starts to detach from what people are actually feeling. That is where synthetic realness begins.

People often describe the modern social media experience as hollow, performative, or overly curated because it has been reorganized around measurable engagement rather than depth. In UX, synthetic realness emerges when teams rely on simplified user profiles and performance dashboards instead of sustained contact with how people actually experience the product. User feedback gets condensed into survey scores, usability summaries, and presentation slides rather than encountered in its full context.

As AI-generated content scales, this problem intensifies. Algorithmic systems will flood platforms with material calibrated to perform. Without new metrics, platforms will reward whatever sustains engagement, even as meaning weakens. As posts, comments, and captions are written to signal status and alignment, semantic fidelity declines. The language circulating on the platform may still feel compelling, but its grounding in lived experience will steadily weaken.

## When Constraints Made Meaning Possible

This wasn’t always inevitable. The early internet and the first wave of social media operated within tighter constraints, with fewer platforms, clearer norms, and lower velocity. Attention was scarce in a way that functioned as a boundary, and meaning emerged because those limits filtered noise and slowed amplification.

Reality drift accelerates when those limits dissolve. High-entropy information environments overwhelm sensemaking. In response, ranking algorithms, ad targeting systems, and growth metrics begin steering what gets amplified. As selection pressure shifts toward measurable engagement, more content competes for attention. The user experience fragments because what people see is shaped less by shared context and more by whatever performs best within the system.

## Why the Average User Doesn’t Exist

Another problem hiding inside current UX metrics is the assumption that people process information in roughly the same way. They don’t. Some are energized by novelty, while others need depth to stay engaged. Some can tolerate dense, fast-moving streams of information, while others fatigue quickly when their attention is pulled in multiple directions. Modern engagement metrics tend to collapse these differences, treating behavior as interchangeable and optimizing toward an average user who doesn’t really exist.

Designs that perform well in aggregate can disproportionately exhaust certain users, even as dashboards look healthy. As feeds speed up and interfaces become more responsive, people begin to feel out of sync with their own pace, scrolling through experiences that move faster than they can meaningfully absorb. Over time, this produces a feeling of ambient loneliness, where connection is constant yet attunement is missing, and nothing quite settles long enough to feel real.

## Language, Compression, and AI Alignment

This dynamic becomes more consequential as AI systems are trained directly on human language, because those systems increasingly treat language as ground truth. But language is not a transparent window into human intent. It is a compressed interface, shaped by perception, memory, emotion, and context after cognitive filtering has already occurred. In high-entropy environments, what survives into words is increasingly influenced by time pressure, distraction, and depleted attention long before a model encounters it.

When users are cognitively depleted, their language becomes thinner, shorter, more reactive, and less reflective. To an AI system, this still registers as valid input, even though the distortion originates within human cognition before the data is ever collected. If models learn from that input without accounting for cognitive state, they will reproduce the patterns created by overload.

Better models alone will not fix this. If inputs reflect exhaustion, outputs will scale it. What’s required is a deeper understanding of language as the interface between lived experience and the systems built on top of it.

## The Drift Beneath the Interface

Taken together, these distortions point beyond interface design and toward the incentive structures shaping it. When systems reward legibility over truth, design becomes the work of making distortion functional. Engagement can rise while satisfaction falls. Optimization continues, and each feedback loop widens the gap between representation and lived experience.

Over time, users respond by withdrawing. They open apps out of habit, scroll without interest, and post less. Disengagement spreads long before any collapse appears in the metrics, because the numbers can remain stable even as the experience thins out. Once that shift has taken hold, no amount of additional optimization can restore meaning, because the conditions that made meaning possible have already been stripped away.
