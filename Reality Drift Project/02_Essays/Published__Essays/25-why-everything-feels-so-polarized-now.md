# Article 25

## Why Everything Feels So Polarized Now

*Originally published March 26, 2026*

> “The map keeps updating even after the territory disappears.”

Illustrated television news studio with a “Breaking News” screen and city skyline backdrop, symbolizing how modern media systems amplify signals and contribute to polarization in the digital information age.

Everyone agrees something has changed in the tone of public life. There's a widespread sense, difficult to articulate but hard to dismiss, that something broke over the last decade and never quite reset. Now more than ever, people ask why everything feels performative, why social media feels hollow, and why nothing seems to mean what it used to. Conversations feel more brittle, disagreements escalate faster, and even minor issues spiral into moral conflict. Politics is the obvious example, but the same dynamic shows up even in interactions that used to be unremarkable.

The common explanation is that people have simply become more ideological or more tribal. But that explanation misses something important. What has changed is the informational environment those beliefs form inside. Polarization is what happens when shared context begins to break down, and it is what we can expect from a scaled digital information environment that is optimized for attention instead of understanding.

## What the System Selects For

Once engagement becomes the dominant metric, content begins competing inside a selection environment. Ideas are filtered, amplified, and retained based on how well they perform against measurable algorithmic metrics. Over time, this creates a subtle but powerful pressure where only the forms of information best adapted to the system can survive. Conflict, certainty, and emotionally legible narratives outperform nuance because they are easier to process and faster to spread. This is the selection mechanism operating as designed. Under these conditions, polarization is the stable outcome to be expected.

This same dynamic also reshapes the nature of disagreement. In a shared informational environment, people can interpret the same underlying reality differently. But once selection pressures fragment the inputs themselves, disagreement no longer happens within a common world. It emerges between increasingly incompatible representations of reality, where people are not just disagreeing about reality, but reasoning correctly from different representations of it.

For most of the twentieth century, information moved through constrained systems. Newspapers had editorial gatekeeping, broadcast news operated across a limited number of channels, and the pace of information was slow enough that most audiences shared roughly the same reference points. Those constraints acted as anchors. They limited the range of possible interpretations and maintained a shared informational baseline.

Algorithmic distribution gradually bypassed those stabilizing structures. Information has become faster, more fragmented, and more reactive. The boundaries that once filtered incoming information into the public sphere weakened. Media has always been self-referential, where media responds to media, but where older forms were slow and constrained enough to allow reality to occasionally break through, modern systems are fast and amplified enough that it rarely does.

## The Cognitive Cost of a Fragmented World

In this new environment, over-optimized content leaves behind a sense of hollowness and synthetic realness. Instead of shared events, people encounter viral clips, moralized headlines, simplified villains, and emotionally charged talking points. This information flows through as urgent and authentic, like a glimpse into reality. But it is only partial representations optimized to travel quickly through networks, not to preserve context.

Each layer of mediation references the last rather than the underlying event, and recursive mediation replaces direct contact with reality. People end up largely reacting to representations of events rather than the events themselves, and that shift introduces a hidden cognitive cost. In a fragmented media environment, every piece of content demands interpretation. Each headline, post, or viral clip leaves unanswered questions: What actually happened? What is this explanation missing? Is this representative or misleading? And this is the part we forget. Most of this interpretive work happens below conscious awareness, so we don’t experience it as active analysis, but as a vague, accumulating cognitive burden.

Filter fatigue becomes a natural adaptation to algorithmic culture, where the informational environment overwhelms independent evaluation, and people stop assessing incoming information on its own terms. Society begins relying on shortcuts such as trusted sources, identity cues, and familiar narratives that can reduce the cognitive load. At the individual level, this is a rational response to information overload, as the mind is not built to sustain this level of continuous interpretation.

But this carries a deeper consequence, as once group-level filters become the primary lens through which people interpret information, polarization accelerates. Different communities begin relying on different narrative shortcuts, different trusted interpreters, and different symbolic cues. At this point, the disagreement is no longer primarily about facts, but a divergence in the framework people use to interpret reality itself.

This dynamic forms part of a larger feedback loop. Optimization pressures amplify emotionally engaging content, and at scale, the constraints that once stabilized meaning through contextual cues weaken. The resulting landscape fills with synthetic representations of reality. The task of interpretation grows more demanding, and so cognitive fatigue pushes people toward even more simplified group-level filtering, which reinforces the cycle.

Each pass deepens the pattern, producing stronger narratives, more emotionally legible conflicts, and less trust in outside interpretation. Over time, different communities begin operating within partially separate symbolic ecosystems. From inside each one, the world appears coherent, but across them, reality begins to fracture.

The interesting thing is that this deeper structural pattern shows up everywhere, just under different names. In tech, we refer to it as enshittification, where platforms optimize for extraction until the product hollows out. In institutions it surfaces as metric gaming and bureaucratic drift. In AI, we talk about it as reward hacking and hallucination. And more than anything we feel it in everyday life, as culture becomes more performative, optimized, and artificial. These all describe systems that optimized at scale and, in doing so, weakened the constraints that bind consequences to reality. They continue to function even as their connection to that reality erodes.

## How Systems Turn Inward

Niklas Luhmann’s concept of autopoietic communication systems helps to explain the structural reasons for why these self-reinforcing cycles are hard for our modern systems to escape. Luhmann argued that modern institutions don’t interact with reality directly, but through their own internal communications. Media responds to media coverage, politics responds to political messaging, and commentary responds to commentary. Systems reproduce themselves through their own feedback loops rather than through contact with the world those processes are meant to describe.

Under normal conditions, external constraints and slower feedback from reality keep those loops loosely anchored. Correction is possible because something outside the system can still exert pressure on it. But once those constraints weaken, the system loses its main mechanisms for self-correction. Communication begins referencing prior communication rather than underlying conditions, and when recursive communication accelerates faster than stabilizing feedback from reality, the system risks becoming operationally closed. It starts running entirely on its own prior outputs, with no mechanism left to force contact with the world outside.

## After Polarization

Seen as a whole, the challenges of modern civilization are not random, but part of a recursive cycle, and if the dynamics are structural, the implications are difficult to avoid. Problems generated at the level of system incentives cannot be resolved solely through better behavior within the system. Appeals to civility, better information, or improved dialogue operate downstream of the mechanism producing the conditions they are trying to fix.

Polarization, then, may not be the endpoint, but a transitional phase in a system that is gradually losing its ability to maintain a shared relationship to reality at all. It is a structural feature of a modern society where hyper-optimized information scales faster than shared meaning can stabilize. And in environments saturated with emotionally amplified content, exhaustion tends to follow fragmentation. Irony culture, detachment, and the familiar modern sense that everything feels fake, in many ways, are products of the same dynamic.

Part of what makes this hard to address is that the people best positioned to understand it are working in separate rooms. Machine learning engineers focused on parameters, compute, and loss functions rarely talk to cultural theorists studying meaning collapse and digital alienation. One community has the mechanistic explanation, the other has the phenomenological one, but neither has both. The deeper issue stops being disagreement. It becomes the fact that the informational environment has drifted away from the conditions that allow shared reality to hold together and the frameworks that could explain why are just as fragmented as the environment they’re trying to describe.
