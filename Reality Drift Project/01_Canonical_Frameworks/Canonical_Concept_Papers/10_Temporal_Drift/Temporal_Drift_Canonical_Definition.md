# Temporal Drift

Part of the Reality Drift framework (2023–2026) by A. Jacobs.

## Canonical Definition

Temporal Drift is a structural condition in which the alignment between systems and real-world time degrades, causing representations, decisions, and experiences to operate on timelines that no longer match current conditions.

As this condition develops, systems remain responsive and functional, but their timing no longer matches the realities they are meant to track. Feedback arrives too late, updates occur too quickly, or coordination breaks across timelines. The system continues to operate, but loses temporal grounding.

## What Makes It Distinct

Temporal Drift differs from ordinary delay or inefficiency. The issue is not simply speed, but the loss of temporal coherence.

Most systems assume that feedback arrives in time to guide correction and that different parts of the system are operating within a shared frame. Temporal Drift begins when those assumptions break down. Updates continue, decisions move forward, and the system may remain active, but its internal timing no longer matches the reality it is meant to track.

The result is persistent misalignment across time.

## Mechanism

Temporal Drift emerges from the interaction of scale, mediation, and optimization:

- **Acceleration of representation**: Systems update models faster than underlying reality changes  
- **Delay of consequence**: Feedback arrives after decisions have propagated  
- **Desynchronization across layers**: Different components operate on different update cycles  
- **Compression of time into indicators**: Real-world processes are reduced into periodic metrics or snapshots  

As systems scale, decisions often move faster than consequences can return. The system keeps updating itself from metrics and reports, while the real effects unfold more slowly elsewhere. Over time, the gap between system time and consequence time widens.

## How It Shows Up

Temporal Drift produces recognizable patterns:

- Decisions are made using outdated or lagging data  
- Rapid updates create instability without improving outcomes  
- Systems overfit to short-term signals while missing long-term shifts  
- Coordination breaks as actors operate on different timelines  

At the experiential level, Temporal Drift is often felt before it is recognized. Time feels compressed, attention shifts more rapidly, and urgency accumulates. Events continue unfolding, but the relationship between action, consequence, and timing becomes harder to track.

## Cross-Domain Effects

Temporal Drift emerges across domains.

### AI / Technology

Models update rapidly while training data, evaluation, and real-world feedback lag behind. Outputs remain coherent but reflect misaligned temporal context.

### Institutions / Organizations

Reporting cycles, decision timelines, and real-world outcomes operate on different cadences, producing delayed or ineffective correction.

### Markets / Economics

Short-term optimization dominates while long-term consequences accumulate outside decision horizons.

### Culture / Media

Content cycles accelerate, compressing attention and collapsing distinctions between events, trends, and narratives.

### Human Cognition

Individuals experience fragmentation of attention and difficulty maintaining temporal orientation under high-frequency input environments.

## Theoretical Context

Temporal Drift extends the Reality Drift framework into the dimension of time.

- **Reality Drift** describes loss of alignment with reality  
- **Recursive Compression** explains how representations propagate and scale  
- **Constraint Collapse** describes loss of corrective force  

Temporal Drift identifies how these dynamics become unstable across time. Compression accelerates updates, constraint delays correction, and recursion amplifies misaligned signals. The system remains functional, but gradually falls out of sync with reality.

## Practical Implications

Temporal Drift cannot be eliminated, but it can be constrained. Without intervention, systems begin optimizing for immediacy over accuracy, feedback becomes less useful, and coordination across time weakens.

Mitigation focuses on restoring temporal alignment. This means shortening feedback loops where possible, slowing decisions when consequences need time to appear, and matching measurement cycles to the real processes being measured. The goal is restoring synchronization.

## In One Sentence

Temporal Drift is the structural misalignment between system time and real-world time, causing systems to remain active and responsive while operating out of sync with the conditions they are meant to track.
