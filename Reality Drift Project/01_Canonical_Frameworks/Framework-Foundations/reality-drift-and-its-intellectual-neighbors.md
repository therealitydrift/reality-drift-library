## Abstract

New conceptual frameworks are often challenged through comparison to earlier traditions. This process can be useful because it situates new models within intellectual history and clarifies where overlap exists. It can also become reductive when comparison is used to collapse distinction rather than illuminate it. Reality Drift shares conceptual terrain with multiple traditions in systems theory, media theory, cybernetics, institutional critique, and epistemology. However, overlap does not imply equivalence. This paper situates Reality Drift among its nearest intellectual neighbors and clarifies where it converges, where it diverges, and what it contributes as a distinct structural model.

## Why Intellectual Lineage Matters

No conceptual framework emerges in isolation. Every serious model inherits vocabulary, assumptions, and methods from earlier thinkers. Acknowledging this is not a weakness. It strengthens the framework by placing it within an intelligible lineage. At the same time, lineage can create interpretive shortcuts. Critics often use prior thinkers to imply that a framework contains nothing new. This move can obscure what is structurally distinct. The purpose of this paper is not to claim originality in absolute terms, but to specify the exact contribution Reality Drift makes within an already crowded conceptual landscape.

## Gregory Bateson and Patterned Difference

Reality Drift shares important affinities with the work of Gregory Bateson, particularly his emphasis on relational patterns and recursive systems. Bateson understood intelligence and communication as processes shaped by difference, feedback, and context. This overlap is foundational because Reality Drift also treats systems as patterned relational structures rather than isolated objects.

The distinction is that Bateson focused primarily on how systems organize and maintain coherence, while Reality Drift focuses on how systems preserve coherence even after their relationship to reality weakens. In this sense, Reality Drift can be understood as extending Bateson into conditions of degraded correction.

## Marshall McLuhan and Mediated Environments

The work of Marshall McLuhan is another clear neighbor. McLuhan’s central insight was that media environments shape human perception before content is consciously evaluated. Reality Drift shares this concern with mediation, especially in environments saturated by symbolic layers and technological abstraction.

The difference is that McLuhan focused on the transformative effects of media environments themselves, while Reality Drift focuses on what happens when those environments begin recursively referencing their own outputs. The shift is from media shaping perception to representations shaping each other.

## Charles Goodhart and Proxy Failure

Reality Drift strongly overlaps with Charles Goodhart in its treatment of metrics and proxies. Goodhart’s law captures a critical mechanism: when a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure. This is one of the clearest structural precursors to Reality Drift.

The distinction is scope. Goodhart identifies a local optimization failure. Reality Drift generalizes this into a broader ecology of representation where not only metrics, but language, procedures, benchmarks, and institutional logic can drift while retaining operational legitimacy. Goodhart describes one mechanism. Reality Drift describes the wider pattern.

## Niklas Luhmann and Self-Referential Systems

The systems theory of Niklas Luhmann offers another strong parallel. Luhmann described institutions as self-referential systems that reproduce themselves through internal communication. This is deeply aligned with Reality Drift’s concern for systems that continue operating through recursive internal logic.

The divergence is again diagnostic. Luhmann described self-reference as a normal feature of modern systems. Reality Drift isolates the point at which self-reference becomes decoupled from corrective reality constraints. It is less a general theory of systems and more a theory of what happens when those systems lose their grounding.

## Jean Baudrillard and Simulation

The work of Jean Baudrillard is often the closest point of confusion. Baudrillard argued that representations increasingly replace reality and create systems of simulation. Reality Drift shares this concern with symbolic substitution and mediated perception.

The distinction is important. Reality Drift does not make a metaphysical claim that reality has disappeared. It makes an operational claim that systems can continue functioning through weakened representations. Baudrillard describes symbolic dominance. Reality Drift describes survivable misalignment.

## Julian Jaynes and Cognitive Structuring

The work of Julian Jaynes matters here because of his attention to the historical evolution of consciousness and internal narration. Reality Drift shares an interest in how symbolic systems shape internal experience, especially under conditions of increasing abstraction.

The difference is level of analysis. Jaynes focused on consciousness itself. Reality Drift focuses on the systems that mediate and structure consciousness in modern environments. Where Jaynes explains the architecture of mind, Reality Drift explains the architecture surrounding it.

## What Reality Drift Adds

Reality Drift contributes a specific structural distinction that is not fully captured by these neighboring traditions. Its central claim is that systems can preserve operational continuity while losing alignment with the realities they are designed to track. This allows misalignment to survive without obvious failure. That continuity is the key.

This creates a diagnostic category that links optimization, mediation, institutional logic, semantic fidelity, and proxy dependence into a unified structural condition. It is not a replacement for these earlier traditions. It is a synthesis with a narrower and more contemporary diagnostic focus.

## Conclusion

Reality Drift belongs to an existing intellectual lineage, but it should not be reduced to it. Its value depends on understanding both its inheritance and its distinctions. Like many frameworks, it stands on earlier conceptual foundations. What matters is not whether it has predecessors, but whether it isolates a pattern that has become increasingly important under modern conditions. The argument of this paper is that it does.
