## Abstract

Conceptual frameworks weaken when their boundaries are unclear, but they also weaken when their criteria for application remain undefined. A framework may have strong conceptual distinctions while still being vulnerable to overextension if there are no minimum conditions for identifying its presence. Reality Drift is especially susceptible to this problem because it describes a broad structural pattern that can appear across institutions, technologies, and cognitive systems. This paper establishes the minimum criteria required for identifying Reality Drift. Its purpose is to preserve diagnostic precision by clarifying what conditions must exist before the framework applies.

## Why Criteria Matter

A framework without criteria risks becoming interpretively loose. Once a concept can be applied to any frustrating, inefficient, or dysfunctional system, it loses structural specificity. Reality Drift depends on preserving a narrower threshold. Not every difficult system is drifting. Not every abstraction produces misalignment. The framework applies only when a specific combination of conditions emerges. Establishing these conditions protects the framework from semantic inflation and preserves its usefulness as a diagnostic tool.

## Criterion One: Operational Continuity

The first requirement for Reality Drift is operational continuity. The system must still be functioning in some recognizable way. It must continue producing outputs, maintaining legitimacy, or sustaining its internal processes. This criterion is essential because drift is not the same as collapse. If a system has visibly failed or broken down, the framework no longer describes its primary condition. Drift refers to systems that continue operating despite weakening alignment. The continuity is part of the concealment.

## Criterion Two: Weakening Corrective Feedback

The second requirement is degradation of corrective feedback. A system must have lost some of its ability to detect and respond to misalignment. This may occur through delayed feedback, abstracted reporting structures, distorted incentives, or recursive mediation. The key issue is that error no longer reliably produces correction. When corrective loops weaken, drift becomes survivable. Without this condition, systems can still self-correct before misalignment compounds.

## Criterion Three: Proxy Substitution

The third criterion is proxy substitution. This occurs when indicators, metrics, models, or symbolic representations begin replacing direct contact with the reality they are meant to track. This does not mean proxies are inherently bad. All complex systems require them. The issue is when proxies become operationally sufficient even as their descriptive fidelity weakens. At this point, the system increasingly organizes itself around the representation rather than the underlying condition.

## Criterion Four: Survivable Misalignment

The fourth criterion is survivability under misalignment. The system must be able to continue functioning while wrong. This is one of the defining features of Reality Drift. In many older systems, being wrong created immediate consequences that forced correction. In modern systems, layers of abstraction often absorb those consequences. This allows misalignment to persist for long periods without visible breakdown. If misalignment cannot survive, drift cannot stabilize.

## Criterion Five: Legitimacy Persistence

The fifth criterion is persistence of legitimacy. The system must continue being recognized as valid, competent, or authoritative despite growing detachment from reality. This legitimacy may come from institutional trust, procedural compliance, benchmark success, or symbolic coherence. This matters because legitimacy helps preserve continuity. It prevents external pressure from forcing recalibration. In many cases, legitimacy is the mechanism that allows drift to deepen.

## Criterion Six: Increasing Mediation

The sixth criterion is increasing mediation between the system and the reality it tracks. This means more layers of abstraction, translation, summarization, automation, or symbolic compression between direct conditions and operational response. Mediation itself is not drift, but increasing mediation raises the probability of fidelity loss. When these layers multiply faster than corrective mechanisms can stabilize them, drift becomes more likely.

## Threshold Logic

Not every system that meets one of these criteria is drifting. Reality Drift should be understood as threshold-dependent. Multiple criteria must be present simultaneously. A system may be complex without drifting, inefficient without drifting, or highly mediated without drifting. The framework becomes applicable when operational continuity, weakened correction, proxy substitution, survivable misalignment, legitimacy persistence, and increasing mediation begin reinforcing one another.

This threshold logic is what distinguishes Reality Drift from general dysfunction.

## Conclusion

Reality Drift is not a loose metaphor for modern frustration. It is a bounded structural condition with identifiable requirements. These criteria provide the minimum threshold for applying the framework responsibly. Their purpose is to preserve its precision across domains. As the framework expands into institutional analysis, AI systems, and semantic environments, these criteria become increasingly important. They provide the conditions under which drift can be recognized before it becomes normalized.
