## Abstract

Diagnosis without intervention can clarify structural problems but often leaves systems unchanged. If Reality Drift describes a recurring condition of operational continuity under declining alignment, then the question of correction becomes unavoidable. This paper examines the mechanisms that can reduce, interrupt, or reverse drift across symbolic, institutional, and technological systems. It argues that drift is not inevitable, but that its correction requires restoring the conditions under which reality can effectively constrain representation. These mechanisms are not universal solutions. They are structural counterweights designed to increase fidelity.

## Why Correction Matters

Reality Drift is not merely a descriptive framework. If it identifies real patterns of survivable misalignment, then its practical relevance depends partly on whether those patterns can be interrupted. Without a theory of correction, the framework risks being treated as diagnosis without consequence. This does not mean every drifting system can be restored, but it does mean the structural conditions that produce drift can be modified. Correction begins by understanding that drift is sustained not by visible failure, but by the weakening of corrective constraint.

## Restoring Direct Feedback

One of the strongest counter-drift mechanisms is restoring direct contact between systems and the realities they are meant to track. Drift intensifies when layers of abstraction buffer systems from consequence. Reintroducing direct feedback reduces this buffering. In practical terms, this may involve reducing unnecessary mediation, shortening feedback cycles, or increasing exposure to first-order conditions rather than relying exclusively on summarized representations. The closer a system remains to reality, the harder it becomes for misalignment to survive undetected.

## Proxy Auditing

Because many drifting systems organize themselves around proxies, regular proxy auditing is essential. This means examining whether the indicators being optimized still accurately represent the underlying conditions they were designed to measure. Proxy audits are not simply technical reviews. They are structural checks on whether the system’s symbolic layer remains grounded. When proxies begin diverging from lived, operational, or material reality, intervention must occur before the divergence becomes normalized.

## Semantic Fidelity Checks

In language systems, one of the most important counter-drift mechanisms is semantic fidelity checking. This involves evaluating whether meaning remains intact across transformations such as summarization, paraphrasing, retrieval, or recursive generation. Surface coherence is often insufficient because it can conceal subtle degradation. Fidelity checks restore pressure on the system to preserve intent, nuance, and contextual accuracy. As AI-mediated communication expands, this mechanism becomes increasingly important.

## Constraint Recovery

Many systems drift because they lose the constraints that once enforced correction. These constraints may be procedural, environmental, institutional, or material. Constraint recovery involves reintroducing limits that make error visible and consequential again. In some cases this means slowing systems down. In others it means restoring human judgment, increasing transparency, or reducing optimization pressure. The key principle is that systems require real friction in order to remain aligned.

## Provenance Preservation

Provenance functions as a stabilizer because it preserves the origin, sequence, and transformation history of information. In drifting systems, provenance often weakens as representations circulate and recursively reference one another. Restoring provenance allows systems to reconnect outputs to their source conditions. This is especially important in AI systems, institutional reporting, and distributed knowledge environments. Provenance does not guarantee alignment, but it increases the visibility of how alignment may have degraded.

## Human Reintroduction

Some forms of drift accelerate when human interpretation is removed too aggressively. Reintroducing human judgment at key decision points can act as a corrective mechanism, especially in domains where nuance, ambiguity, or ethical complexity exceed what proxies can reliably capture. This does not imply that human judgment is inherently superior. Humans drift too. The point is structural diversity. Systems become more fragile when all correction is internal to the same symbolic architecture.

## Compression Limits

Modern systems increasingly depend on compression to scale. Information is summarized, categorized, abstracted, and automated. Compression is necessary, but beyond certain thresholds it increases the probability of fidelity loss. One counter-drift strategy is establishing limits on recursive compression. This means preserving access to richer underlying layers rather than relying entirely on increasingly simplified representations. Systems that can move back down the stack are more capable of self-correction.

## Conclusion

Counter-drift mechanisms do not eliminate complexity, mediation, or abstraction. Their purpose is narrower. They restore the capacity of reality to constrain the systems that represent it. This is the central principle. Drift becomes possible when representation becomes operationally sufficient without remaining descriptively faithful. Correction therefore requires increasing the pressure of reality on representation.

The practical importance of Reality Drift depends not only on recognizing misalignment, but on understanding how systems can be designed to resist it. These mechanisms are early attempts at that task.
