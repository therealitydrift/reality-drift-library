## The Basic Pattern

Robert Rosen spent much of his career studying a deceptively simple question:

> How do systems successfully navigate reality before events occur?

His answer was that living systems do not merely react to the world.

They anticipate it.

Organisms continuously construct internal models of their environment and use those models to guide future behavior.

In Rosen's framework, anticipation is not an optional feature of intelligence.

It is a fundamental requirement for survival.

Systems must act based on representations of reality before reality fully reveals itself.

---

## What Rosen Saw

Rosen argued that intelligent behavior depends upon internal models.

A system cannot evaluate every possibility directly.

Instead, it develops representations that simplify complexity.

These representations allow:

- prediction

- planning

- adaptation

- coordination

- decision making

Without models, action becomes impossible.

The future arrives too quickly.

Complexity becomes overwhelming.

Systems survive by relying on representations of reality rather than reality itself.

---

## Anticipatory Systems

Rosen used the term **anticipatory system** to describe systems that contain models of themselves and their environment.

These models allow action to occur before outcomes are known.

The organism acts.

The environment responds.

The model updates.

The cycle repeats.

This process creates a crucial dependency.

The system's success increasingly depends on the quality of its internal representations.

When those representations remain aligned with reality, anticipation works.

When they do not, failure becomes increasingly likely.

---

## What Rosen Did Not Yet See

Rosen died in 1998.

He never encountered:

- social media algorithms

- recommendation systems

- large language models

- generative AI

- synthetic media

- AI agents

- digital twins

- large-scale predictive systems

The systems Rosen studied used internal models.

Modern societies increasingly operate through them.

This distinction matters.

The question is no longer simply:

> How do systems anticipate reality?

The question increasingly becomes:

> What happens when societies become dependent on representations that are multiple layers removed from reality itself?

---

## From Anticipation to Representation Dependence

The Reality Drift framework extends Rosen's insights into contemporary informational environments.

Modern systems increasingly operate through models.

Organizations use forecasts.

Governments use indicators.

Platforms use algorithms.

AI systems use embeddings.

Institutions use dashboards.

Representations increasingly mediate action.

The model remains useful.

The dependency grows.

The system becomes increasingly reliant on representations it can no longer directly verify.

---

## The Drift Principle

Within the Reality Drift framework, this process is described through the Drift Principle:

> When systems become increasingly dependent on representations, maintaining alignment between those representations and reality becomes progressively more important and more difficult.

The model survives.

The prediction survives.

The decision survives.

The correspondence becomes uncertain.

This extends Rosen's concerns into environments he never experienced.

Rosen explained why systems require internal models.

Reality Drift examines what happens when entire societies become dependent on models, metrics, simulations, forecasts, and symbolic representations.

---

## Modern Examples

Many contemporary phenomena can be understood through this lens.

### AI Alignment

AI systems operate through representations of language, knowledge, and objectives.

Performance increasingly depends on whether those representations remain grounded.

### Economic Forecasting

Organizations act on models of future conditions.

The forecast survives.

Reality changes.

### Institutional Decision Making

Policies increasingly emerge from indicators, reports, and predictive systems.

The representation guides action.

The underlying reality becomes harder to observe directly.

### Digital Platforms

Algorithms anticipate user behavior and optimize accordingly.

The model becomes increasingly influential in shaping the environment it attempts to predict.

### Reality Drift

Systems remain operational while becoming progressively more dependent on representations whose relationship to reality becomes increasingly uncertain.

---

## Robert Rosen and Reality Drift

Rosen identified a critical feature of intelligent systems before the rise of modern digital environments.

He understood that anticipation depends on internal models.

What he did not fully witness was the emergence of societies increasingly organized around models, simulations, forecasts, algorithms, and AI systems.

Reality Drift can be understood as an attempt to describe what happens when representational dependence becomes a dominant feature of civilization itself.

The challenge is no longer simply creating models.

The challenge is maintaining fidelity between models and the realities they were designed to anticipate.
