## Terrence Deacon and the Structure of Meaning

Terrence Deacon proposed a fundamentally different way of understanding how meaning emerges in biological and cognitive systems. In his view, meaning does not arise primarily from the information a system receives but from the constraints that shape how that information is interpreted.

In this sense, meaning emerges from a form of negative space. Constraints determine what possibilities are excluded, which impulses are inhibited, and which interpretations are ruled out.

These invisible boundaries give thought and perception their structure. They stabilize distinctions between signals, contexts, and interpretations, allowing organisms and cultures to maintain coherent patterns of meaning.

When these constraints remain intact, systems can interpret the world with relative stability. When they weaken, however, the structures that hold meaning together begin to erode.

---

## Constraint Collapse

The Reality Drift framework extends Deacon’s insight by describing what happens when the constraints that stabilize meaning begin to degrade.

Modern informational environments generate continuous streams of novelty, variation, and recombined language. Algorithmic systems amplify possibility while weakening the boundaries that once limited interpretation.

Distinctions blur, interpretations multiply, and systems begin producing patterns without the structures required to stabilize them.

Within the Reality Drift framework this condition is described as **constraint collapse**. As constraints weaken, **semantic fidelity** declines — representations, language, and models may remain fluent and internally coherent while becoming progressively less grounded in the realities they were meant to describe.

The **Drift Principle** captures the pressure driving this process: when informational entropy rises faster than systems can maintain structure, the boundaries that once stabilized meaning weaken first.

---

## Meaning in High-Entropy Environments

When constraints weaken, meaning becomes harder to stabilize. Systems continue generating interpretations and representations, but the structures that once grounded them begin to fail.

Within the Reality Drift framework this produces recognizable dynamics:

- **Optimization Trap** — systems optimize measurable indicators rather than underlying meaning  
- **Filter Fatigue** — individuals must constantly evaluate competing streams of information  
- **Synthetic Realness** — expressions of authenticity become engineered and repeatable  
- **Reality Drift** — systems remain active while gradually losing alignment with real-world conditions  

In this sense, Reality Drift can be understood as a modern extension of Deacon’s insight. When informational environments outpace the constraints that stabilize interpretation, meaning itself becomes fragile.
