## Overview

**AI alignment** refers to the challenge of ensuring that artificial intelligence systems behave in ways that reflect human goals, values, and intentions.

As AI systems become more capable and autonomous, researchers and engineers increasingly focus on how to ensure that these systems produce outcomes that remain beneficial, predictable, and aligned with real-world objectives.

Alignment problems often arise when an AI system optimizes a measurable objective that does not fully represent the broader goals it is intended to achieve. In these situations, the system may technically satisfy its instructions while producing unintended or undesirable outcomes.

Because AI systems learn patterns from large datasets and optimize specific performance targets, maintaining alignment between system behavior and real-world intentions has become one of the central challenges in modern AI research.

---

## How AI Alignment Relates to Reality Drift

Within the **Reality Drift framework**, AI alignment problems can be understood as a form of systemic misalignment between representation and reality.

AI systems often operate by optimizing measurable training objectives, evaluation benchmarks, or performance metrics. When these indicators become imperfect proxies for real-world goals, systems may fall into an **Optimization Trap**, where improving measurable outputs no longer guarantees alignment with the intended outcome.

As AI systems generate increasingly fluent language and reasoning patterns, maintaining **Semantic Fidelity** becomes critical. Semantic fidelity refers to the degree to which language, models, and representations remain faithfully connected to the realities they describe. When systems produce coherent outputs that are not reliably grounded in real-world conditions, meaning itself can begin to drift.

At the same time, advanced AI systems may produce responses that simulate understanding, empathy, or reasoning. This contributes to environments of **Synthetic Realness**, where outputs appear convincingly human-like even when underlying comprehension or grounding remains limited.

As AI-generated information expands across digital environments, users must increasingly interpret, evaluate, and verify large volumes of machine-generated content. This can produce conditions of **Filter Fatigue**, where the cognitive effort required to navigate information environments grows steadily.

Together these dynamics can create situations in which AI systems remain highly capable according to internal benchmarks while their outputs become progressively shaped by optimization objectives rather than by stable connections to real-world conditions.

Within the framework, this broader pattern can be understood as **Reality Drift within AI-mediated systems**.

---

## Where This Pattern Appears

These dynamics can appear across many AI-driven environments:

- Language models optimizing benchmark performance rather than real-world reasoning accuracy  
- AI-generated content that convincingly simulates human communication  
- Automated decision systems guided primarily by measurable performance targets  
- Recommendation algorithms shaping information environments through engagement optimization  

In these environments, AI systems may appear increasingly capable while the relationship between system outputs and real-world alignment becomes more difficult to interpret.
