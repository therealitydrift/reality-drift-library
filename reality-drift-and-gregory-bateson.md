## Gregory Bateson and the Pattern That Connects

Gregory Bateson argued that living systems remain coherent not because of isolated pieces of information but because of what he called **“the pattern that connects.”** Minds, organisms, and cultures maintain stability by preserving relationships between signals, context, and feedback.

When those relationships remain intact, systems can interpret their environment accurately and respond appropriately. When they degrade, communication begins to distort. Systems start misreading their own cues, responding to the wrong patterns, or losing the contextual distinctions that once guided behavior.

Bateson warned that environments can generate more information than a system can meaningfully integrate. When this happens, the organism loses its relationship to context. Distinctions flatten, feedback loops destabilize, and the system begins reacting to noise as if it were meaningful input.

---

## The Mechanism: Drift and Recursive Compression

The Reality Drift framework extends Bateson’s insight by describing how minds maintain the patterns he described.

Human cognition does not process every piece of information independently. Instead, it compresses experience into patterns and models that simplify the surrounding environment. These models allow the mind to interpret signals, maintain context, and respond coherently to complex situations.

This process is recursive. The mind not only compresses information into patterns, it also builds models of those patterns, continually updating its interpretation of the world. This recursive compression allows perception, narrative, identity, and meaning to remain relatively stable even in changing environments.

But this process has limits. When environments generate information faster than these models can integrate it, compression becomes more aggressive and less precise. Distinctions flatten, context weakens, and feedback becomes harder to interpret. The relationships that once stabilized meaning begin to slip out of synchronization.

The **Drift Principle** describes this breakdown. When informational entropy rises faster than systems can maintain coherent compression, internal representations begin drifting away from the realities they were meant to track.

---

## When the Pattern Falls Out of Sync

Modern digital environments dramatically increase informational entropy. Continuous feeds, algorithmic systems, and networked media produce volumes of signals that exceed the integrative capacity of individuals and institutions.

Within the Reality Drift framework this produces recognizable dynamics:

- **Optimization Trap** — systems optimize measurable indicators rather than underlying reality  
- **Filter Fatigue** — individuals must constantly evaluate competing streams of information  
- **Synthetic Realness** — expressions of authenticity become engineered and repeatable  
- **Reality Drift** — systems remain active while gradually losing alignment with real-world conditions  

In this sense, Reality Drift can be understood as a modern expression of Bateson’s warning. When informational environments accelerate beyond the capacity of systems to maintain coherence, **the pattern that connects falls out of synchronization.**
