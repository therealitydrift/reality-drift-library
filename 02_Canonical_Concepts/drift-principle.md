# Drift Principle

Part of the Reality Drift framework (2023–2026) by A. Jacobs

## Canonical Definition

The Drift Principle describes how meaning and alignment degrade when environmental complexity grows faster than a system’s capacity to compress and stabilize information.

All symbolic systems — minds, institutions, technologies, and cultures — must compress complex environments into simplified internal representations in order to function. When the volatility or informational entropy of the environment exceeds that compression capacity, representations begin to lose fidelity.

Drift is the result: a gradual loss of coherence between representations and the realities they are meant to describe.

## Core Relation

The Drift Principle can be expressed as a simple information-theoretic relation:

Drift = Entropy / Compression

Where:

**Entropy**  
The uncertainty, noise, unpredictability, and informational volatility present in an environment.

**Compression**  
The process by which systems reduce complexity into simplified representations, models, narratives, or metrics in order to coordinate and act.

When entropy rises faster than compression capacity, drift increases and fidelity declines.

## What Drift Produces

As drift intensifies, systems exhibit characteristic effects:

- degradation of meaning and contextual grounding  
- unstable or fragmented representations  
- increased reliance on simplified signals or proxies  
- difficulty maintaining coherent identity or direction  
- growing divergence between internal models and external conditions

## Environmental Drivers

Modern digital environments dramatically amplify entropy through:

- continuous information streams
- algorithmic unpredictability
- synthetic and AI-generated media
- fractured temporal rhythms
- rapid recombination of symbols and narratives

These conditions push cognitive, cultural, and technological systems toward higher drift states.

## Relationship to Other Reality Drift Concepts

Within the Reality Drift framework, the Drift Principle describes the **core mechanism** that generates several observable patterns:

**Reality Drift**  
The systemic condition in which institutions and cultures remain operational while gradually losing alignment with lived reality.

**Filter Fatigue**  
The cognitive exhaustion produced by continuous filtering in high-entropy environments.

**Synthetic Realness**  
The perceptual inversion in which optimized representations feel more authentic than reality.

**Optimization Trap**  
The institutional dynamic in which measurable efficiency replaces meaningful outcomes.

**Constraint Collapse**  
The structural failure regime in which systems lose the ability to invalidate their own representations.

## Applications

The Drift Principle applies across multiple domains:

**Cognition**  
attention fragmentation, burnout, identity instability

**Culture**  
memetic repetition, synthetic realness, narrative instability

**Institutions**  
metric substitution, optimization traps, bureaucratic drift

**Technology and AI**  
model drift, synthetic content loops, co-cognitive systems

## In One Sentence

The Drift Principle states that systems lose alignment with reality when environmental entropy grows faster than their ability to compress and stabilize information.
